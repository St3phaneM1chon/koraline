# LLQP - LIFE LICENCE QUALIFICATION PROGRAM

## Overview
- **Administering body:** Canadian Insurance Services Regulatory Organizations (CISRO)
- **Website:** https://www.cisro-ocra.com
- **Effective date of harmonized LLQP:** January 1, 2016
- **Scope:** All provinces and territories EXCEPT Quebec (which uses a separate AMF regime)
- **Purpose:** Standardized pre-licensing education for life insurance and A&S insurance agents

## LLQP Modules (4 modules)
1. **Module 1: Life Insurance** -- principles, types of policies, taxation, underwriting
2. **Module 2: Accident & Sickness Insurance** -- disability, critical illness, health insurance
3. **Module 3: Segregated Funds & Annuities** -- investment products, guarantees, taxation
4. **Module 4: Ethics & Professional Practice** -- duty of care, regulatory framework, compliance

## LLQP Process (National Standard)
1. **Register** with Canadian Insurance Participant Registry (CIPR) -- maintained by provincial regulators
2. **Complete LLQP course** from a CISRO-approved course provider (e.g., CSI, Oliver's Learning, IFSE Institute, Learnedly, PNC Learning)
3. **Pass course provider's internal exams** for each module
4. **Course provider certifies you on CIPR** -- triggers eligibility to write provincial licensing exams
5. **Write provincial LLQP licensing exam** (administered by Durham College in most provinces)
6. **Pass all 4 modules** -- pass mark is **60%** in each module
7. **Obtain sponsorship** from a licensed life insurance company or MGA
8. **Apply for provincial license** through the provincial regulator
9. **One-year window:** Must write provincial exams within 1 year of course completion

## Key Rules
- **Portability:** LLQP exam certificate is valid across all common law provinces -- no need to re-test if moving provinces
- **Reinstatement:** If unlicensed for 2+ years, must retake LLQP course and exams (varies by province, typically 1-2 years)
- **Criminal record check:** Required in most provinces for new applicants
- **E&O Insurance:** Required in some provinces (varies)
- **Failed exam:** May rewrite twice, then must wait 3 months (NB rule; similar in other provinces)
- **Pilot questions:** 5 pilot questions per exam (not scored)

## OSFI Guidelines Relevant to Life Insurance

### Guideline E-13: Regulatory Compliance Management (RCM)
- **Current version:** 2014 (revised from 2003 Legislative Compliance Management guideline)
- **URL:** https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/guidance/guidance-library/regulatory-compliance-management-rcm-guideline-2014
- **Applies to:** All Federally Regulated Financial Institutions (FRFIs), including life and P&C insurers
- **Key expectations:**
  - Enterprise-wide Regulatory Compliance Management (RCM) framework
  - Three lines of defence model (operational management, compliance function, internal audit)
  - Board and senior management accountability
  - Risk-based approach to compliance
  - Identification and assessment of regulatory compliance risks
  - Monitoring and reporting
  - "Regulatory requirements" defined broadly: laws, rules, regulations, and prescribed practices
- **Relationship to Integrity & Security Guideline:** E-13 is referenced in OSFI's 2024 Integrity and Security Guideline (Principle 4)

### Guideline B-3: Sound Reinsurance Practices and Procedures
- **Current version:** 2025 (final revised version issued February 11, 2022; took effect January 1, 2025)
- **URL:** https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/guidance/guidance-library/sound-reinsurance-practices-procedures-guideline-2025
- **Applies to:** All Federally Regulated Insurers (FRIs) -- life and P&C
- **Key principles:**
  - Reinsurance as risk management tool (reduce risk, stabilize solvency, increase underwriting capacity)
  - Reinsurance Risk Management Policy (RRMP) required
  - Board oversight of reinsurance arrangements
  - Adequate and effective reinsurance arrangements
  - Conditions for capital credit under LICAT and MCT
  - Stress testing of reinsurance arrangements
  - Foreign FRI restrictions: reinsurance back to home office through affiliates generally not recognized
  - Cross-reference: Guideline B-10 (Outsourcing) if reinsurance intermediaries used

### Guideline B-20: Residential Mortgage Underwriting Practices and Procedures
- **Current version:** 2018 revision (original 2012)
- **URL:** https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/guidance/guidance-library/infosheet-residential-mortgage-underwriting-practices-procedures-guideline-b-20
- **Applies to:** FRFIs that grant mortgages (banks and trust companies primarily, but also relevant to mortgage insurers)
- **Key requirements:**
  - Minimum Qualifying Rate (MQR) "stress test"
  - Borrowers must demonstrate ability to absorb interest rate or income changes
  - Uninsured mortgages: minimum 20% down payment
  - Loan-to-value, loan-to-income, debt-service ratios
- **Insurance relevance:** Affects mortgage insurance companies (CMHC, Sagen, Canada Guaranty) which are regulated as P&C insurers

### Life Insurance Capital Adequacy Test (LICAT)
- **Current version:** 2025
- **URL:** https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/guidance/guidance-library/life-insurance-capital-adequacy-test-guideline-2025
- **Applies to:** Federally regulated life insurance companies and fraternal benefit societies
- **Purpose:** Measures capital adequacy of life insurers; one of several OSFI indicators for financial condition
- **Key features:**
  - Chapter 10: Credit for Reinsurance (links to B-3)
  - Available Capital vs. Base Solvency Buffer
  - Insurance risk, market risk, credit risk, operational risk components
  - Supervisory target and minimum ratios
  - Regulatory Notice on Adjustments (May 22, 2025)
- **Provincial adoption:** BCFSA adopted LICAT for BC-regulated life insurers (via Capital Requirements Regulation)

### Corporate Governance Guideline

# ANTI-MONEY LAUNDERING (PCMLTFA/FINTRAC)

## Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act
- **Citation:** S.C. 2000, c. 17
- **URL:** https://fintrac-canafe.canada.ca/act-loi/1-eng
- **Administering body:** Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada (FINTRAC)
- **Website:** https://fintrac-canafe.canada.ca

## Application to Insurance
- **Life insurance companies** are reporting entities under the PCMLTFA
- **Life insurance brokers and agents** are also reporting entities
- Does NOT apply to general (P&C) insurance intermediaries

## Key Obligations for Life Insurance Entities
1. **Register with FINTRAC** (where applicable)
2. **Compliance program:** Must establish and maintain a compliance program including:
   - Compliance officer appointment
   - Written policies and procedures
   - Risk assessment
   - Training program
   - Effectiveness review (every 2 years)
3. **Client identification:** Verify client identity when:
   - Lump-sum payment of $100,000+ towards annuity or life insurance policy
   - Opening account
   - Establishing business relationship
4. **Record keeping:** Prescribed records, retained for 5 years
5. **Reporting obligations:**
   - **Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs):** When reasonable grounds to suspect transaction related to ML/TF
   - **Large Cash Transaction Reports:** Cash of $10,000+
   - **Terrorist Property Reports (TPRs):** If property known/suspected to be owned by terrorist entity
   - **Listed Person/Entity Property Reports (LPEPRs):** New as of October 1, 2025
6. **PEP/HIO determinations:**
   - Must determine if clients are Politically Exposed Persons (foreign or domestic), Heads of International Organizations, or close associates/family
   - Triggered at $100,000+ lump-sum payment for annuities/life insurance
7. **Third party determination:** Not required if information record kept on beneficiary in connection with life insurance policy where $10,000+ to be remitted
8. **Beneficial ownership:** New requirements to flag discrepancies with federal registry (regulatory amendments 2025)

## FINTRAC and OSFI Cooperation
- FINTRAC and OSFI share information under PCMLTFA and OSFI Act
- FINTRAC is primary AML/ATF compliance assessor for FRFIs
- OSFI supervises financial condition; FINTRAC supervises AML compliance

## Recent Developments (2025-2026)
- October 1, 2025: New FINTRAC requirements effective including LPEPR regime, title insurer regulation, WLATM acquirer regulation
- Budget 2024 amendments: Information-sharing framework between reporting entities
- Cost recovery: FINTRAC moving toward cost recovery from regulated entities (similar to OSFI)

# PRIVACY (PIPEDA)

## Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act
- **Citation:** S.C. 2000, c. 5
- **URL:** https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/p-8.6/
- **Applies to:** Commercial activities, including insurance
- **Provincial equivalents:** AB (PIPA), BC (PIPA), QC (Private Sector Privacy Act) -- deemed substantially similar

## Key Principles for Insurance (10 Fair Information Principles)
1. **Accountability** -- insurer/agent is responsible for personal information
2. **Identifying purposes** -- purpose of collection must be identified at or before collection
3. **Consent** -- knowledge and consent required (exceptions exist for beneficiary investigations)
4. **Limiting collection** -- only collect what is necessary
5. **Limiting use, disclosure, retention** -- use only for identified purposes
6. **Accuracy** -- keep information accurate, complete, up-to-date
7. **Safeguards** -- protect with appropriate security measures
8. **Openness** -- policies and practices must be readily available
9. **Individual access** -- right to access and correct personal information
10. **Challenging compliance** -- ability to challenge compliance

## Insurance-Specific Privacy Issues
- **Underwriting:** Collection of health, financial, lifestyle information -- must have consent
- **Claims investigation:** Insurer may collect/use information without consent if necessary for investigation of breach of agreement or contravention of law (s. 7(1)(b))
- **Beneficiary information:** Collection justified for policy administration
- **Litigation privilege:** Protected from disclosure (confirmed in Lizotte v. Aviva, 2016 SCC 52)
- **Breach reporting:** Mandatory breach notification requirements

# KEY JURISPRUDENCE

## A. Duty of Good Faith (Uberrimae Fidei)

### 1. Whiten v. Pilot Insurance Co., 2002 SCC 18
- **Citation:** [2002] 1 SCR 595
- **Issue:** Bad faith claims handling; punitive damages
- **Holding:** SCC upheld $1 million punitive damages against insurer for bad faith denial of fire claim. Insurer's conduct in denying claim was "planned and deliberate" and constituted breach of duty of good faith.
- **Principle:** Insurance contracts are clothed in the duty of good faith (uberrimae fidei) throughout the claims handling process. Bad faith by insurer can result in punitive damages.
- **URL:** https://www.canlii.org/en/ca/scc/doc/2002/2002scc18/2002scc18.html

### 2. Coronation Insurance Co. v. Taku Air Transport Ltd., 1991 CanLII 16 (SCC)
- **Citation:** [1991] 3 SCR 622
- **Issue:** Duty of disclosure; material misrepresentation
- **Holding:** Insurance places a heavy burden on applicants to provide full disclosure of all information relevant to the risk the insurer is assuming.
- **Principle:** The duty of utmost good faith requires full and complete disclosure. Material non-disclosure can void the policy.

### 3. Bhasin v. Hrynew, 2014 SCC 71
- **Citation:** [2014] 3 SCR 494
- **Issue:** Good faith as organizing principle in contract law
- **Holding:** SCC recognized good faith as a "general organizing principle" of Canadian contract law and established a duty of honest performance requiring parties to be honest with each other in contractual performance.
- **Principle:** While an insurance-specific case was not at issue, this landmark case underpins all contractual good faith analysis in Canada, including insurance contracts.

### 4. C.M. Callow Inc. v. Zollinger, 2020 SCC 45
- **Citation:** [2020] 3 SCR 908
- **Issue:** Expansion of duty of good faith; misleading conduct
- **Holding:** SCC expanded the duty of honest performance -- parties cannot lie or knowingly deceive about matters directly linked to contractual performance. Misleading conduct includes lies, half-truths, omissions, and even silence.
- **Principle:** Directly applicable to insurance: insurers and insureds cannot mislead each other about matters related to the insurance contract.

### 5. Trial Lawyers Association of BC v. Royal & Sun Alliance Insurance Co. of Canada, 2021 SCC 47
- **Issue:** Insurer's duty to defend; withdrawal after discovering policy breach
- **Holding:** Addressed waiver by conduct and estoppel in context of insurer that defended for years then withdrew after discovering insured's breach.
- **Principle:** Complex interplay between insurer's duty to defend and insured's duty of disclosure.

## B. Misrepresentation and Non-Disclosure

### 1. Mohammad v. The Manufacturers Life Insurance, 2020 ONCA 57
- **Issue:** Scope of disclosure obligation
- **Holding:** The disclosure obligation is not limited to answering questions in the application correctly; it extends to volunteering all material information. Innocent non-disclosure of material facts can invalidate a life insurance contract.
- **Principle:** Material misrepresentation or non-disclosure -- whether intentional or innocent -- can relieve insurer of coverage obligations.

### 2. Davies v. AIG Insurance Company of Canada, 2024 ONCA 509
- **Issue:** Material misrepresentation
- **Holding:** Court confirmed that material misrepresentation, whether intentional or not, can relieve insurer of obligation to provide coverage.
- **Principle:** Reinforces the objective materiality test in modern jurisprudence.

### 3. Costanza v. Desjardins Financial Security Life Assurance Company, 2023 ONCA 54
- **Issue:** Duty of disclosure and good faith
- **Holding:** Reaffirmed the duty of good faith and honesty including the disclosure requirement in insurance applications.

### 4. Taylor v. London Assurance Corp., 1935 CanLII 2 (SCC)
- **Citation:** [1935] SCR 422
- **Issue:** Subjective element of materiality test
- **Holding:** Non-disclosure or misrepresentation must have induced the insurer to enter into the contract.
- **Principle:** Two-part test: (1) was the information material? (2) did it induce the contract?

### 5. Van Schilt v. Gore Mutual Insurance Co., 1988 CanLII 2974
- **Issue:** Uberrimae fidei; answers in application form
- **Holding:** In contracts of utmost good faith, the fact that a question is asked in the application makes the answer material. Duty on applicant to use reasonable diligence to ensure answers are correctly written.

## C. Insurable Interest

### 1. Kosmopoulos v. Constitution Insurance Co., 1987 CanLII 75 (SCC)
- **Citation:** [1987] 1 SCR 2
- **Issue:** Insurable interest of sole proprietor in incorporated company's assets
- **Holding:** SCC held that the sole shareholder had an insurable interest in the company's assets, even though the company was a separate legal entity. The Court adopted a broad, liberal approach to insurable interest.
- **Principle:** Insurable interest is defined broadly -- any economic interest that would be prejudiced by the peril insured against suffices. The Court rejected the narrow "legal or equitable interest in the property" test.
- **URL:** https://www.canlii.org/en/ca/scc/doc/1987/1987canlii75/1987canlii75.html

### 2. Caldwell v. Stadacona Fire & Life Insurance Co., 1883 CanLII 42 (SCC)
- **Citation:** (1883) 11 SCR 212
- **Issue:** Timing of insurable interest
- **Holding:** Insurable interest must exist at the date of acquisition of the policy AND at the time of the loss.
- **Principle:** Establishes the dual-timing requirement for insurable interest in property insurance.

### 3. Fordorchuk v. Car & General Insurance Corp., 1931 CanLII 313 (AB QB)
- **Issue:** Insurable interest during foreclosure
- **Holding:** Insurable interest continues to exist after foreclosure proceedings because the mortgagor remains the owner and in possession.

## D. Beneficiary Designation Disputes

### 1. Moore v. Sweet, 2018 SCC 52
- **Citation:** [2018] 3 SCR 303
- **Issue:** Irrevocable beneficiary designation vs. prior agreement; constructive trust
- **Holding:** SCC held that despite an irrevocable beneficiary designation under the Ontario Insurance Act, the Act does not oust the contractual or equitable rights of another person. Applied constructive trust (unjust enrichment) to award proceeds to ex-wife who had paid premiums based on agreement with deceased.
- **Principle:** An irrevocable beneficiary designation does not give absolute entitlement to policy proceeds if another person has contractual or equitable rights.
- **URL:** https://www.canlii.org/en/ca/scc/doc/2018/2018scc52/2018scc52.html

### 2. Oldfield v. Transamerica Life Insurance Co. of Canada, 2002 SCC 22
- **Issue:** Public policy; innocent beneficiary of criminal insured
- **Holding:** SCC held it is NOT against public policy to permit an innocent beneficiary to obtain life insurance proceeds where the insured accidentally died during a criminal act.
- **Principle:** The forfeiture rule does not extend to innocent designated beneficiaries.
- **URL:** https://www.canlii.org/en/ca/scc/doc/2002/2002scc22/2002scc22.html

### 3. Royal Bank of Canada v. North American Life Assurance Co., 1996 CanLII 219 (SCC)
- **Citation:** [1996] 1 SCR 325
- **Issue:** Life insurance beneficiary designation vs. creditors in bankruptcy
- **Holding:** Where family members are designated as beneficiaries, life insurance proceeds are exempt from execution/seizure under the Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act. The designation constitutes a "settlement" under the BIA.
- **Principle:** Life insurance with designated family beneficiaries is protected from creditors -- sound policy to protect family dependents.
- **URL:** https://www.canlii.org/en/ca/scc/doc/1996/1996canlii219/1996canlii219.html

### 4. Lalonde v. Sun Life Assurance Co. of Canada, 1992 CanLII 39 (SCC)
- **Issue:** Revocation of beneficiary under group life insurance
- **Holding:** Whether parties intended to make beneficiary designation revocable; implications of special life insurance legislation.

## E. Policy Interpretation (Contra Proferentem)

### 1. Ledcor Construction Ltd. v. Northbridge Indemnity Insurance Co., 2016 SCC 37
- **Citation:** [2016] 2 SCR 23
- **Issue:** Interpretation of "faulty workmanship" exclusion in builders' risk policy
- **Holding:** SCC held that the exclusion excluded only the cost of redoing the faulty work itself, not the resulting damage. Applied general rules of contract interpretation; stated contra proferentem would have led to same result.
- **Principle:** Standard form insurance contracts are interpreted as a matter of law (not mixed fact and law). Contra proferentem is a rule of last resort -- applies only after general interpretive principles fail to resolve ambiguity.
- **URL:** https://www.scc-csc.ca/case-dossier/info/sum-som-eng.aspx?cas=36452

### 2. Progressive Homes Ltd. v. Lombard General Insurance Co. of Canada, 2010 SCC 33
- **Citation:** [2010] 2 SCR 245
- **Issue:** Duty to defend; CGL policy interpretation; defective workmanship
- **Holding:** SCC established national framework for interpretation of CGL policies:
  1. Give effect to clear, unambiguous language
  2. If ambiguous, apply general contract principles consistent with reasonable expectations
  3. **Only if ambiguity persists**, apply contra proferentem against insurer
  4. Corollary: coverage provisions interpreted **broadly**; exclusion clauses **narrowly**
- **URL:** https://www.canlii.org/en/ca/scc/doc/2010/2010scc33/2010scc33.html

### 3. Non-Marine Underwriters, Lloyd's of London v. Scalera, 2000 SCC 24
- **Citation:** [2000] 1 SCR 551
- **Principle:** When language of policy is unambiguous, court gives effect to clear language, reading contract as a whole.

### 4. Consolidated-Bathurst Export Ltd. v. Mutual Boiler and Machinery Insurance Co., 1980 CanLII 10 (SCC)
- **Citation:** [1980] 1 SCR 888
- **Principle:** Foundational SCC case on contra proferentem in insurance -- ambiguity resolved against insurer.

### 5. Emond v. Trillium Mutual Insurance Co. (SCC, recent)
- **Issue:** Clear exclusion language
- **Holding:** When insurance policy language is clear and unequivocal, courts must apply with full force and not neutralize exclusions through interpretation rules.
- **Principle:** Contra proferentem and reasonable expectations doctrines do NOT apply absent genuine ambiguity.

## F. Agent/Broker Liability

### 1. Fine's Flowers Ltd. v. General Accident Assurance Co. of Canada, 1977 CanLII 1182 (ON CA)
- **Issue:** Broker duty of care; "full coverage" request
- **Holding:** Ontario Court of Appeal held broker liable when insured asked for "full coverage" but the policy placed did not cover the loss that occurred. The broker should have identified foreseeable risks, obtained appropriate insurance, or warned of gaps.
- **Principle:** The "intelligent insurance agent" standard -- a broker who undertakes to arrange coverage must identify foreseeable risks, obtain appropriate coverage, or clearly warn of gaps. Liability arises in both contract and negligence.

### 2. Fletcher v. Manitoba Public Insurance Co., 1990 CanLII 59 (SCC)
- **Citation:** [1990] 1 SCR 702
- **Issue:** Insurer's duty to advise of optional coverage
- **Holding:** SCC held insurer's employees negligent in failing to advise the insured of availability of underinsured motorist coverage when the insured had requested maximum coverage.
- **Principle:** Brokers owe a duty of care to advise clients of available coverage options. The Supreme Court confirmed brokers' advisory duty when:
  - Special relationship of trust exists
  - Client relies on broker's expertise
  - Broker has opportunity to advise

### 3. Canada Brokerlink Inc. v. Patterson, 2006 CanLII 50894 (ON SCDC)
- **Issue:** Duty to inform of better pricing
- **Holding:** Court expanded duty to include advising client of possibility of better pricing from competitors.

### 4. Bronfman v. BFL Canada Risk & Insurance Services Inc., 2013 ONSC 5372
- **Issue:** Inadequate coverage limits
- **Holding:** Broker negligent for not adequately probing needs and advising on appropriate limits/endorsements relative to insureds' lifestyle and asset profile.

### 5. Beck v. Johnston (BC Court of Appeal)
- **Issue:** Failure to advise of policy exclusions
- **Holding:** Insurance brokers can be sued for professional negligence if they fail to properly advise clients of limits and exclusions of their policies.

# NATIONAL REGULATORY BODIES

## Canadian Council of Insurance Regulators (CCIR)
- **Website:** https://www.ccir-ccrra.org
- **Purpose:** Inter-jurisdictional association of insurance regulators; promotes efficient and effective regulatory system
- **History:** Founded 1914 (western provinces); Ontario joined 1917; all provinces by 1989
- **Jurisdictional Overview:** https://www.ccir-ccrra.org/CanadianInsuranceRegulatoryFrameworks-JurisdictionalOverview
- **Members:** All provincial/territorial regulators plus OSFI (federal)

## Canadian Insurance Services Regulatory Organizations (CISRO)
- **Website:** https://www.cisro-ocra.com
- **Purpose:** Forum for consistent qualifications and conduct standards for insurance intermediaries
- **Key functions:**
  - LLQP administration (curriculum, exam preparation materials)
  - Canadian Insurance Participant Registry (CIPR) -- national database of licensed intermediaries
  - Canadian Insurance Regulators Disciplinary Actions (CIRDA) database
  - FINTECH/INSURTECH Advisory Hub (joint with CCIR)
- **Contact:** cisro-ocra@fsrao.ca (Secretariat hosted by FSRA)

## Insurance Bureau of Canada (IBC)
- **Website:** https://www.ibc.ca
- **Purpose:** Industry association for home, auto, and business insurers
- **Focus:** P&C insurance policy and advocacy

## Canadian Life and Health Insurance Association (CLHIA)
- **Website:** https://www.clhia.ca
- **Purpose:** Industry association for life and health insurers
- **Key function:** Conducts surveys of licensing requirements across provinces

## Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI)
- **Website:** https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca
- **Purpose:** Federal prudential regulator for banks, insurance companies, trust companies, pension plans
- **Guidance library:** https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/guidance/guidance-library
- **Key for insurance:** Regulates solvency/capital, corporate governance, risk management of federal insurers
